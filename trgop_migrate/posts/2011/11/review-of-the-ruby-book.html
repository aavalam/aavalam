Review of "The Book of Ruby"

November 1 2011, 2:06 PM  by Victor Goff

Review of "The Book of Ruby" by Huw Collingbourne

TL;DR Good book, not without its pain points, and not totally for the beginner of Ruby, though there are some definite juicy parts that I would share with them, some of which are noted in a list near the end of this post.

  This review is written by Victor Goff.


  First things I noticed about the book is the table of contents and it is looking very good. It is well organized and chock full of goodies. As far as the index goes it is very good also.  

  The second thing I did was flipped through the book looking at the code snippets (It is a programming book after all) and noticed that apparently quite a few people had provided code, as the style is pretty consistently not consistent with indentation, and even block styles. It goes deeper, even the use of parentheses around puts calls is not consistent, sometimes they are there (actually very often) and sometimes they are not. Seldom it is to show the occasional places that it is required to get away from ambiguity.

  However, upon starting to read the book, and Mr. Collingbourne's notice that this book "does not preach on matters of style.", indeed, it sometimes seems to not follow its own style.  Having completed the reading of this book, I can confirm that for me, it was somewhat distracting.

  There was some other distracting things about the book, but I think (hope) in further editions they may be alleviated. Two samples are below.

  Page 126: Problem: Using Integer to prove assignment of a variable doesn't affect the object ID is not a proof. It is good though that shortly after the examples, he does mention that Integers are 'special'. It would be better if a String or some other mutable object were used to provide the point earlier, instead of using integers though.  He isn't the only one that does this example... I have seen it in a lot of places in the web, and each gives me the same bad taste.

  Related to the above, he even suggests that the object_id of 10 is 21, and 23 is the object_id of 11, and that it may vary. This is not the case, the object_id will not vary, and can lead students down the wrong path, the wrong assumptions if they aren't familiar with what he explains starting on the following page. And that is that Integers are special. Their ID doesn't change.

  So it is things like this that cause me to not trust the other information. Even though the next page he reverses his statement of 'may be' to how Integer is special, and those ids never change.

  There was also information I expected to be there in the Digging Deeper sections, though they were mostly tasty, that was missing. For example 'next' for blocks wasn't discussed, and allocate method wasn't covered. Just a few notable exclusions. But understandably, you can't cover everything in a book.  

  If you are comfortable with Ruby, and willing to proof the concepts here, it is very likely you will learn some new things. It will likely help to make your understanding of Ruby more complete.  One such area of "proof this" exists in the Digging Deeper section for Chapter 6, where it is presented that:

  A friend of mine asked me if I couldn't write something different in this review... some more positive oriented points.

  I hope that this review doesn't portray the message that I thought the book was terrible, or bad.  It really isn't.  I just can't see handing this book to someone new to Ruby and stating "This is the book for you!  Read it and work through the presented examples, and you will understand Ruby."  It is possible that this would happen, but because of the inconsistencies, the lack of experience of that person to make informed opinions of where style is important, some of the potential goose chases presented in the book, and the 'proof this' areas, without walking through some of these ares, makes it a book that may confuse more than enlighten.

  I won't list all of the sections where a beginner to Ruby would benefit, but a couple of notable inclusions would be:
 * A quick overview of Documenting Ruby with RDoc that gives a quick and not-in-depth introduction to RDoc.
 * The same for Apendix  B, a quick set of instructions for "Installing MySQL for Ruby on Rails"     

 * Speaking of Rails, about 24 pages or so of Ruby On Rails, just enough to get your teeth into it

The long and the short of it.  If you are just learning Ruby there are less frustrating books to purchase.  Having said that, if you have some experience with Ruby, and are wanting a good reference book, and wanting to exercise your skepticism, it is a good book.  It definitely forced me to learn a bit more, and that is never a bad thing.

  I will use it as a reference, it will stay in my library of books on Ruby.  It has value, and it has insights.  There is no question of that.  I will likely even read it again.
